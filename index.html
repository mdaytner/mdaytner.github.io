Introduction

In this tutorial, we'll go over the basics of performing data science through a step by step investigation of the viability of investing in the S&P500 to build a retirement fund.

Saving for retirement is the elephant in the room. Many Americans put it off for later, believe saving for retirement is futile, or have no idea how to effectively pull it off through investments. According to tradingeconomics.com, the US Dollar's annual inflation rate averaged 3.23% per year from 1921 to 2021. This means that each year, every dollar in your wallet loses about 3.23% of the purchasing power it held the prior year. While it may seem like only a small fraction chipped off, inflation will seriously add up and eat away at the value of idle dollars over time. When aiming to save for retirement, it's best to have your saved dollars work for you over time via investments rather than let inflation eat away at their future value.

Figuring out how to invest your hard earned money to buuld the foundation for a comfortable retirement can often sound easier said than done. There are a plethora of investment vehicles, but the most well known is the stock market. Many people shy away from investing in the stock market due to the perceived risk and/or lack of understanding how it works.

"How do I pick the right stocks?"
"The stock market is only for rich people, right?"

As it turns out, retirement investment gurus actually tend to preach a one-size-fits-all approach to long term investing: index funds. An index fund is basically a basket of stocks that tracks and mirrors certain stock market indices such as the notable S&P500, an index comprised of the top 500 publicly traded American companies. Index funds are passively managed which means that an investor doesn't have to lift a finger—simply put money into the fund and let it do the work of tracking its index. Since index funds are essentially collections of many individual stocks, this takes the burden of researching and selecting individual stocks off of the investor.


"Is this too good to be true?"
"Surely there's a catch."
"Doesn't investing in some of everything defeat the purpose of trying to grow wealth? Surely some stocks in an index fund will go up, but some have to go down too, right?"

In the following data science adventure, we are going to answer some of these questions! We'll determine whether simply dumping money into an index fund such as the S&P500 is a viable route to take for the average American looking to sip on piña coladas on the beach at 65.

2. Tidying the Data

Now that we've successfully scraped our data into a Pandas DataFrame, let's clean it up to make it easier to work with down the line.

At this stage, we'd determine how to deal with missing data entries if there were any. Data can be missing from datasets for a variety of reasons. Narrowing down a good idea of why a piece of data is missing helps us understand how to best deal with it. A great resource about the kinds of missing data and ways of dealing with it can be found here: https://www.mastersindatascience.org/learning/how-to-deal-with-missing-data/#:~:text=When%20dealing%20with%20missing%20data,reasonable%20guesses%20for%20missing%20data.&text=Removing%20data%20may%20not%20be,result%20in%20a%20reliable%20analysis.

Fortunately in the S&P500 dataset we're using here, there are no missing values. Now we can make sure the data is properly usable for our exploratory data analysis.

The main thing that stands out in our DataFrame that could cause an issue when trying to play with our data is the % sign tacked on to each entry in the "Annual% Change" column as seen above. Since it's a non-numerical character, it could get in the way of numerical operations.

# Want to rename the columns to ditch spaces. The column names with spaces are technically tuples of strings which

Taking this observation into account, we can get a much better idea of how the S&P500 behaves over time by analyzing the percent change over time rather than the price. That way we can have a more standardized view of the data. A 6% percent gain in 1930 is much more comparable to a 6% gain in 2020!

A one-time investment of 10,000 dollars is not a very realistic example of saving for retirement. Let's simulate a scenario with regular contributions each year. According to the U.S. Cencus Bureau, the 2018 U.S. median household income was 61,937 dollars. Many resources such as Fidelity, a successful financial services company, recommends Americans save and invest about 15% of their annual income.

For more information about how much you should save for retirement, check here: https://www.fidelity.com/viewpoints/retirement/how-much-money-should-I-save

So for our simulation, we'll invest 15% of our 61,937 dollar income which comes out to 9290.55 dollars per year. This means 52646.45 dollars are leftover for expenses and we want our investments to reach a level such that we can safely withdraw 52646.45 dollars per year indefinitely. We'll a 40 year window to simulate a 40 year working career.

# Make array of investment values each year with added contribution and array of added up contribution values over time
principal = 9290.55
value_array = [principal]
principal_array = [principal]

    principal_array.append(principal_array[-1] + 9290.55)

Wow! The power of compounding returns will make your investments soar over a long time horizon! By investing 15% of the median household income in the S&P500 each year, we were able to amass a total value north of 2 million dollars! The orange line shows only the flat 9290.55 dollar annual contribution which really helps put into perspective how much the power of compounding returns dwarfs contributions in the long haul.

If we assume the same 52646.45 dollars of expenses in retirement, we will want our annual expenses withdrawn to be <= 4% of our total investment value. This means we'll need about 1316161.25 dollars to retire. Given that we reached well over 2 million, We've got a lot of breathing room!

Take special note of how greatly the slope of our value curve increases in the later years of our working career. We just invested the same 9290.55 dollars every year and the growth picked up like crazy to the point where the annual growth of our investment probably came close to our entire income for the year!


5. Conclusion


Is the S&500 a good choice for the average Joe American to invest in to fund their retirement? From our analysis, it is safe to say YES. Historically, money invested in the S&500 has grown by about 7.81% each year adjusted for inflation. By investing 15% of your annual income into the S&500 over 40 years, it is safe to say you will be set. Starting early is instrumental in achieving such a high amount of money over time. For those who are late bloomers in the retirement savings scene, savings rate should be increased to meet their needs by the time they retire.

On the flip side, starting investing in the S&500 at the very start of your career and investing a ludicrous percentage of your income each year can set you up to be financially independent from occupation-earned income at a very young age! Of course it can be very difficult to save so much when making a fairly standard income given America's cost of living.

However, our analysis here shows it is definitely feasible to become a retired multi-millionaire on a standard household income through early and consistent investments into the S&500. Simply "set and forget". Set aside your investments from each paycheck, dump into the S&500, and forget about it. Let the S&500 work its compounding magic and trust the historic average returns to continue.

If we were to take this analysis further, one thing we consider doing is trying to use supervised machine learning to create a linear regression model that could predict the growth of the S&500 in future years. I chose not to take that route since 94 years of S&500 data allowed us to get an average return rate which modeled the actual growth of an investment quite well. For more information on supervised machine learning and linear regression models, look here: https://www.ibm.com/cloud/learn/supervised-learning

A good Python library for making linear regressions is Scikit Learn: https://scikit-learn.org/stable/modules/generated/sklearn.linear_model.LinearRegression.html

To increase the realisticness of our experiment, we could take into account more factors such as changing incomes (i.e. annual raises and increasing savings rate), location/cost of living, etc.


TL;DR: Regularly dumping money into an index fund that tracks the S&500 is a simple and effective way to fund your retirement. Start as soon as possible for the best results!
